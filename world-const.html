Grassroots Foundation: This Constitution recognizes that meaningful global governance can only emerge through grassroots movement from the lowest level of power - the people themselves. When everyone on the planet makes the decision to demand fair governance, reject war, and oppose dictatorship, existing power structures will be compelled to adopt these principles through peaceful, democratic means.

Preamble

We, the people of Earth, recognizing our shared humanity, common destiny, and sacred responsibility to present and future generations, hereby establish this World Constitution to promote peace, justice, equality, environmental sustainability, democratic participation, and shared prosperity for all nations and peoples. We affirm that sovereignty is shared responsibility, that Earth's resources are our common heritage, and that all humans possess inherent and inalienable rights.

Grassroots Foundation: This Constitution recognizes that meaningful global governance can only emerge through grassroots movement from the lowest level of power - the people themselves. When everyone on the planet makes the decision to demand fair governance, reject war, and oppose dictatorship, existing power structures will be compelled to adopt these principles through peaceful, democratic means.

Chapter I: Fundamental Principles

Article 1: Government Prototype Principle

Government Prototype Principle: The global government described in this constitution serves as a prototype for successive levels of governing bodies. This serves as a safety valve for local governance, preventing corruption, abuse and runaway control of resources and people.

Article 2: The Diversity Principle

Diversity as Ultimate Strength: Despite all other indicators, diversity is the ultimate strength. In nature, all things happen every time, everywhere at once. Survival of anything or any one is an event bordering a miracle. Diversity is a phenomenon that allows or even promotes survival or the thriving of any entity.

Universal Application: All governance, social, economic, and cultural structures shall be designed to promote and protect diversity in all its forms - biological, cultural, linguistic, ideological, cognitive, and gender diversity. Diversity is not merely tolerated but actively cultivated as the primary mechanism for human survival and thriving.

Article 2: Gender Diversity Integration

Gender Reality Framework: The idea of genders appears very simple at the surface. But upon closer examination, we can observe a great variety of occurrences. Again, it is inherent to the human species by the nature of diversity. When looking at a sample size of a hundred people, gender is simple: Male or Female. But when looking at the sample size of a million, things become more complex. There are males with female attributes, and females with male attributes. However, at the sample size of ten million, things become even more complex. There are males with female attributes, and females with male attributes in the physical form, as well as in the psychological form.

Integration Solution: The real question is how we, as society, deal with this phenomenon. The recommended solution is simple: Integration. Do not promote or suppress, allow all to integrate as one human being. Current examples show us how we are currently dealing with it: some societies deal with it by annihilate the deviation, some societies deal with it by promoting it ahead of 'regular' sexuality. If you had any doubt about the difference see prosecution of one and the pride parade of the other.

Article 3: Coexistence Approach

Principle of Coexistence: Coexistence is important. Be very conservative of what you output, and very liberal on what you accept. In the modern age, at any age for that matter, it is not feasible to achieve anything by force. This is why it is so important to govern by consent, with the agreement of all parties involved.

Article 4: Youth as Primary Resource

Youth Priority: Our most valuable resource is youth. The future is determined on how we value, how we cherish, how we educate our young. To that effect: Promote scientific education. Promote coexistence. Promote participation on the greater good. Under the age of adulthood: no religious education. Where separation is not possible, the context must be made clear to the young person.

Article 5: Shared Sovereignty and Subsidiarity

Sovereignty is redefined as shared responsibility for the common good. Nations retain authority over local and cultural affairs while committing to global cooperation. Decisions shall be made at the lowest competent level (principle of subsidiarity), with global governance addressing only issues that cannot be effectively handled locally or regionally. No single entity shall possess veto power over global decisions.

Article 6: Peace Without Wars

No More Wars Resolution: This resolution adopts principle of peace on planet. To that effect, demilitarization starts immediately. The steps: withdrawing every foreign military personnel and equipment from every country; unwinding military to level proportional to territory and/or population, to level matching agreed international norms; availing local military to conflict suppression. The suppression mechanism can only be invoked by international body, and only in case of ongoing conflict. No more wars. Only negotiated outcomes.

Military Leadership Restrictions: No person with military background or military involvement shall be permitted to hold leadership or governing roles. Person holding any rank, commanding in any capacity is disqualified. Exception to this rule is personnel deployed in non-combat or supporting role.

Peaceful Resolution: All international disputes shall be resolved through negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or judicial settlement before any enforcement measures. Only negotiated outcomes shall be considered legitimate.

Article 7: Global Communication and Language

Primary International Language: To enable maximum participation in global matters, English shall be primary language for international affairs and inter-area communications, ensuring effective and accessible dialogue between all regions.

Scope Limitation: The language requirement applies only to inter-area communications between different linguistic regions. Local and regional affairs may be conducted in any language chosen by community.

Language Diversity Protection: The language of any tribe, area, or country shall be fully accepted and protected by this Constitution without hindrance. All local languages shall be preserved as cultural heritage with educational support for multilingual competence.

Article 8: Religious Freedom and Secular Governance

Individual Spiritual Freedom: Every person has right to practice their religion or spirituality individually and in community, with freedom of conscience, belief, and access to religious institutions without interference.

Secular Global Governance: No religion shall influence official world affairs or global governance. Religious doctrine shall be separate from political decision-making at the international level, ensuring policies serve all humanity regardless of spiritual beliefs.

No Promotion or Suppression: There shall be no force or incentive to suppress religious practice, and no force or incentive to propagate religion through state mechanisms or public policy.

Inter-Religious Relations: Interactions between religious entities shall be confined to negotiated outcomes and mutual respect, with the state serving as neutral mediator in disputes while protecting religious freedom for all.

Cultural and Spiritual Boundaries: Acknowledging that religious boundaries often coincide with cultural and linguistic boundaries formed by historical human interpretation of divine will, global governance shall approach religious diversity with humility, accepting that no single interpretation holds exclusive access to truth in matters of global governance. Individual spiritual needs are distinguished from effects on collective decision-making.

Chapter II: Government Prototype and Structure

Article 9: Government Prototype and Safety Valves

Government Prototype Principle: The global government described in this constitution serves as a prototype for successive levels of governing bodies. This serves as a safety valve for local governance, preventing corruption, abuse and runaway control of resources and people.

Article 10: Four-Tiered Structure

Executive Branch: Implements Assembly decisions through collective leadership. No single individual has the capacity to fulfill the challenges of global leadership. The post of president is replaced with a round table of decision makers, similar to a round table of knights. The number of decision makers shall be an odd number, no less than five, no more than nine. The members of this group should be selected by elections at regular intervals, if necessary with supervision from a higher level of governing body, ultimately supervision by international governing body.

Legislative Branch: Consists of representative assemblies at each level with proportional representation and citizen participation.

Monitoring Branch: Independent oversight mechanism to prevent corruption, abuse, and ensure transparency across all government operations.

Judicial Branch: Interprets laws and adjudicates disputes through court systems at each level, with mechanisms for appeal to higher levels.

Inter-branch Balance: The four branches act as checks on each other to create a balanced decision-making environment. This serves as a safety valve for local governance, preventing corruption, abuse and runaway control of resources and people. Mechanism should exist for dispute resolution in form of elevation to next higher level body, ultimately supervised by the international governing body.

Article 11: Global Governance - United Earth Assembly

United Earth Assembly (UEA) shall be the primary legislative body with:

  • Proportional representation by population
  • Additional seats for indigenous and marginalized communities
  • Mixed representation combining national delegates with randomly selected citizens
  • No single executive authority or veto power ("No King, No Veto" principle)
  • Leadership through rotating facilitation rather than permanent hierarchy
  • Governance by consent, being conservative in output and liberal in acceptance of diverse perspectives
  • Recognition that force is infeasible in modern governance, requiring agreement of all parties involved

Article 12: Regional and Local Governance

Continental Federations shall coordinate regional development, harmonize policies, and provide intermediate governance between global and local levels. These shall include African, European, Asian-Pacific, American, and other regional groupings determined by geographic and cultural affinities.

Sovereign Local Governments shall preserve language, religion, culture, and legal traditions while operating within the universal rights framework. Nations shall maintain autonomy over internal affairs not requiring global cooperation.

Legislative Branch consists of the United Earth Assembly as the primary law-making body, ensuring broad representation and participatory democracy.

Monitoring Branch serves as an independent oversight mechanism to prevent corruption, abuse, and runaway control of resources and people. This branch provides continuous monitoring of all government operations, ensuring transparency and accountability while serving as a safety valve for local governance.

Judicial Branch interprets laws and adjudicates disputes through the World Court system, ensuring legal consistency and protection of rights across all governance levels.

Inter-branch Balance: The four branches act as checks on each other to create a balanced decision-making environment. Mechanisms exist for dispute resolution through elevation to the next higher level body, ultimately supervised by the international governing body.

Article 13: Leadership Qualifications and Restrictions

No Presidents Rule: No single individual has the capacity to fulfill the challenges of global leadership. The post of president is replaced by a team of individuals, similar to a round table of knights. The members of this group should be selected by elections at regular intervals.

Age Limit Principle: Age limit is imposed on leadership. The global mean retirement age is sixty-four (64). No person should be elected to any governing body at or above this limit, minus the length of term for that particular office. For instance, a governing position for a four-year term cannot elect any person at or above age of 60. This is a necessary arrangement, but wisdom of elders are not lost, as people who are not eligible for office can still hold advisory positions.

Military Personnel Restriction: No military. For leadership and/or governing positions, no person shall be permitted to hold a role if that person has military background or military involvement. Person holding any rank, commanding in any capacity is disqualified. Exception is personnel deployed in non-combating or supporting role.

Cultural Evolution Standard: Cultural evolution requires morphing into a state of mind where qualifications are ahead of any other trait. A knowledgeable/qualified person has severe disadvantage against person with charismatic presence. In many instances the qualified person has disadvantage even if charismatic opponent has no relevant qualifications. This cultural evolution is essential for effective governance and leadership selection, ensuring that expertise and knowledge are prioritized over superficial charisma in democratic processes.

Specialized Agencies shall include:

  • Public Health Agency
  • Environmental Protection Agency
  • Science and Innovation Agency
  • Social Development Agency
  • Digital Governance Agency
  • Disaster Response Agency
  • Ethics Oversight Council (12 independent members with rotating facilitation)

Article 14: Judiciary and Enforcement

World Court and Planetary Court shall adjudicate disputes, ensure compliance, and prosecute crimes against humanity, war crimes, and ecocide. Global Anti-Corruption Tribunal shall handle cross-border corruption cases.

Chapter III: Comprehensive Rights and Freedoms

Article 15: Civil and Political Rights

Citizens shall possess fundamental civil and political rights essential for human dignity and democratic participation. These include freedom of speech, expression, and peaceful assembly; right to privacy, personal autonomy, and data protection; freedom of thought, conscience, religion, and belief; right to participate in government through free, fair, and regular elections; unconditional and expedient due process and fair trial; and right to access information and government transparency.

Article 16: Economic and Social Rights

Citizens shall possess fundamental economic and social rights essential for human dignity and wellbeing. These include the right to adequate standard of living including food, housing, and healthcare; the Planetary Resource Dividend providing universal basic income and services funded by global resource fees; right to universal, inclusive, and multilingual education at all levels; right to work, fair labor conditions, and workplace democracy; right to social security and protection in old age, disability, or hardship; and equal access to opportunity and basic needs for all.

Article 17: Environmental Rights

Citizens possess fundamental environmental rights essential for human survival and planetary health. These include right to a clean, healthy, ecologically balanced, and sustainable environment; right to access clean water, clean air, and healthy food; right to participate in environmental decision-making; right of future generations to planetary integrity and biodiversity; and protection from ecocide and large-scale environmental harm.

Article 18: Digital and Technology Rights

Citizens possess fundamental digital and technology rights essential for modern participation and protection. These include right to internet access and digital inclusion; right to digital privacy and protection from surveillance; right to open-source knowledge and technological innovation; right to algorithmic transparency and accountability; and protection from AI bias and automated decision-making harms. In the age of global communications and artificial intelligence, governance systems must acknowledge and perhaps adapt to new innovations through continuous review, ethical oversight, and integration of beneficial technologies while protecting fundamental human rights.

Chapter IV: Global Economic Framework

Article 19: Planetary Resource Management

Key natural resources of global significance shall be treated as shared commons, including atmosphere, deep seabed, biodiversity hotspots, and polar regions. Global Resource Fees shall be imposed on carbon emissions, deep-sea mining, data extraction, and commons usage to fund the Planetary Resource Dividend. This ensures that all humanity benefits from shared planetary resources while protecting them for future generations.

Article 20: Progressive Global Taxation

A progressive global taxation system shall address wealth inequality and fund universal social services. Natural resource extraction and pollution shall bear external costs. Financial transactions and wealth accumulation shall be progressively taxed to prevent extreme inequality, ensuring fair contribution from all according to ability to pay.

Article 21: Fair Trade and Development

International trade shall promote sustainable development, reduce inequality, and respect environmental and labor standards. Trade privileges shall be tied to transparency, democratic reforms, and human rights compliance. Special consideration and support shall be provided to developing nations to ensure equitable participation in global economic systems.

Article 22: Free Enterprise and Equal Opportunity

Free Enterprise and Equal Opportunity: Free enterprise shall be supported with no discrimination, no preferences, and equal opportunity for all. No human being or enterprise should experience fear or favor in economic activities. The economic framework shall ensure fair competition and prevent monopolistic practices while supporting innovation and entrepreneurship.

No Fear or Favor Principle: The economic system shall be designed so that no human being or enterprise experiences fear of reprisal or receives unfair favoritism. Equal opportunity and fair competition shall be guaranteed for all participants in economic activities.

Article 23: Innovation and Knowledge Commons

Open innovation and knowledge sharing shall be promoted for education and key technologies. Research and development in critical areas shall be globally coordinated. Equitable benefit-sharing shall ensure technological advances serve all humanity.

Chapter V: Environmental Protection and Climate Justice

Article 24: Climate and Environmental Framework

Mandatory net-zero greenhouse gas emissions shall be achieved by scientifically determined targets. Global reserve zones shall protect endangered ecosystems. Ecocide and large-scale environmental harm shall be globally prosecutable offenses.

Article 25: Biodiversity and Ecosystem Protection

Conservation of biodiversity and sustainable resource use shall be mandatory. International environmental agents shall enforce protection measures. Planetary boundaries framework shall be integrated with human rights obligations.

Article 26: Intergenerational Responsibility

All governance decisions shall consider impacts on future generations. A duty to maintain planetary integrity shall be legally binding. Global environmental monitoring and scientific coordination shall guide policy.

Chapter VI: Anti-Corruption and Transparency Framework

Article 27: Citizens' Integrity Chamber

Citizens' Integrity Chamber (CIC) shall be a rotating, lottery-selected citizen panel reviewing high-level decisions. Members shall serve limited terms with comprehensive protection and resources.

Article 28: Radical Transparency

Mandatory publication of all government contracts, budgets, meeting minutes, and lobbying interactions shall be required. Public Ledger Systems using immutable cryptographic logs shall track finances, decision logic, and asset declarations.

Article 29: Whistleblower Protection

Anonymous reporting channels with legal immunity and financial rewards shall be established. Whistleblowers shall receive comprehensive protection and support.

Article 30: Media and Investigative Support

Global Investigative Journalism Trust with firewalled funding and rotating editorial oversight by civil society boards shall be established. Incentives for cross-border investigative collaborations shall be provided.

Article 31: AI Oversight and Ethics

Open-source, auditable algorithms with independent AI Ethics Ombudsman shall govern automated systems. Algorithmic decisions shall be explainable and contestable.

Chapter VII: Participatory Democracy and Citizen Engagement

Article 32: Direct Democracy Mechanisms

Global Referenda on key issues requiring ≥60% turnout and ≥66% approval shall be implemented. Citizen Juries of 100-200 randomly selected people shall provide deliberative guidance on complex issues. These mechanisms ensure direct democratic participation beyond mere voting, allowing citizens to actively engage in governance through deliberative processes and policy-shaping initiatives.

Article 33: Deliberative Democracy

Hybrid Conventions combining national delegates with randomly selected citizens shall address major policy questions. Digital Deliberation Platforms and open-source governance tools shall enable broad participation. These mechanisms ensure that diverse voices are heard and considered in policy-making through structured deliberation rather than simple majority voting.

Article 34: Ethics Veto and Popular Impeachment

Citizen-initiated mechanisms for removing corrupt officials and vetoing unjust decisions shall be established. High thresholds shall prevent abuse while ensuring accountability. These mechanisms include ethics veto powers allowing citizens to block governmental actions that violate fundamental principles, and popular impeachment processes for removing officials who betray public trust.

Article 35: Integrity Education

Mandatory ethics training for public officials and civic education programs shall promote democratic values and anti-corruption awareness. These programs shall be accessible to all citizens and emphasize integrity, transparency, and accountability in public service.

Chapter VIII: Education, Culture, and Knowledge

Article 36: Universal Education

Universal Education Guarantee: Universal, inclusive, multilingual education shall be guaranteed at all levels. Education shall promote peace, sustainability, intercultural understanding, and critical thinking. Educational systems shall evolve culturally to prioritize qualifications and knowledge over charismatic presence, ensuring that qualified individuals are not disadvantaged by those with lesser knowledge but greater personal appeal. This cultural evolution is essential for effective governance and leadership selection.

Secular Education for Minors: Under the age of adulthood, there shall be no religious education provided to young people. Where separation between secular and religious education is not possible in educational systems, the secular nature of education must be made explicitly clear to young persons. This promotes worldly education and critical thinking while respecting individual spiritual development through private means.

Article 37: Cultural Diversity and Heritage

Cultural diversity shall be celebrated with heritage preservation for all peoples. Intercultural exchange programs shall be promoted. Endangered languages and traditions shall receive special protection. This recognizes that cultural diversity is a strength of humanity and that all peoples have the right to preserve their unique heritage while participating in the global community.

Article 38: Global Knowledge Systems

Open-access scientific knowledge and equitable innovation systems shall be established. Traditional and indigenous knowledge shall be respected and integrated with modern science. This creates a comprehensive knowledge ecosystem combining technological advancement with respect for ancestral wisdom and diverse ways of knowing.

Chapter IX: Dispute Resolution and Enforcement

Article 39: Peaceful Resolution

All international disputes shall be resolved through negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or judicial settlement before any enforcement measures. This principle ensures that dialogue and peaceful resolution are prioritized over force and that all parties seek mutually beneficial outcomes through structured conflict resolution processes.

Article 40: Enforcement Mechanisms

The World Assembly may authorize graduated measures including diplomatic pressure, economic sanctions, targeted measures against individuals, and peacekeeping operations to ensure compliance with this Constitution. These measures shall be applied progressively and with emphasis on peaceful resolution before any enforcement actions are taken.

Article 41: Global Peacekeeping Service

A volunteer-based Global Peacekeeping Service shall intervene in cases of genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and ecocide with clear mandates and civilian protection priorities. This service shall operate under strict civilian control principles and focus on protection rather than combat, with intervention as last resort after all peaceful means have been exhausted.

Chapter X: Constitutional Flexibility and Development

Article 42: Amendment Process

This Constitution may be amended through formal processes requiring broad consensus and democratic participation. The amendment process ensures stability while allowing for adaptation to changing global circumstances. Amendments may be proposed by the World Assembly and shall require approval through both legislative supermajority and citizen referendum, ensuring that changes reflect genuine popular will.

Article 43: Review and Evolution

Every five years, a World Constitutional Convention shall review effectiveness and recommend improvements. Every ten years, a comprehensive review shall consider fundamental restructuring if needed. This ensures the Constitution remains relevant and adaptive to changing global circumstances through regular democratic evaluation and evolution.

Article 44: Emergency Powers

During global emergencies, limited temporary powers may be granted with strict time limits, judicial oversight, and citizen approval through referenda within 90 days. Emergency powers shall be narrowly defined, proportional to the threat, and subject to immediate review by independent judiciary to prevent abuse.

Chapter XI: Implementation and Transition

Article 45: Transitional Implementation

A Foundational Charter with pilot federations shall operate independently initially, similar to existing international organizations. This transitional approach allows for gradual implementation and testing of constitutional principles before full adoption, ensuring that systems work effectively through practical experience and adaptation.

Article 46: Incentives and Support

Financial and technical incentives shall encourage developing nations' participation. Early adopter benefits shall include innovation grants and sustainable finance, technology transfer and capacity building, preferential trade and investment access, and priority access to global resources and programs. These measures ensure equitable participation and shared benefits in global governance.

Article 47: Ratification Process

This Constitution shall enter into force when ratified by two-thirds of nations representing at least two-thirds of the world's population through both governmental approval and citizen referenda.

Article 48: Grassroots Movement and Popular Support

Grassroots Foundation: This document has unlikely support in current power structure. The main method of this document to find footing is a grassroots movement. This movement may originate from the lowest level of power: people. If everyone on the planet makes the decision to want fair governance, to want no wars, to want no dictators, current ruling entities have no choice but to adopt the principles outlined in this document.

Popular Adoption Path: The unlikely support from current power structures necessitates direct democratic engagement. Citizens worldwide must organize, advocate, and demand adoption of these governance principles through peaceful, democratic means.

Transition through Popular Will: As public support grows, existing institutions will be compelled to adapt, ensuring that transition to global governance reflects genuine popular sovereignty rather than elite capture.

Article 49: Supremacy and Compatibility

This Constitution shall be the supreme law of the world. National laws inconsistent with its provisions shall be invalidated to the extent of inconsistency. Cultural and religious practices shall be respected unless they violate fundamental human rights.

Chapter XII: Final Provisions

Article 50: Universal Jurisdiction

Crimes against humanity, genocide, war crimes, torture, slavery, and ecocide shall be subject to universal jurisdiction regardless of where committed or by whom.

Article 51: Scientific Cooperation

Global coordination of scientific research for pandemics, climate response, energy transitions, and other existential challenges shall be mandatory. Open-access publishing and equitable benefit-sharing shall be required.

Article 52: Symbolic Elements

The world shall adopt symbols representing unity in diversity, including a flag, anthem, and celebration of World Citizenship Day to promote global identity while respecting local cultures.

Conclusion

Adopted this day by the peoples of the world, united in our commitment to peace, justice, environmental sustainability, democratic participation, and shared prosperity for present and future generations. We affirm that in our diversity lies our strength, in our cooperation lies our security, and in our shared humanity lies our hope.

Glossary

Key terms and concepts used in the World Constitution, defined for clarity and understanding.

A

Algorithmic Transparency - The requirement that automated decision-making systems must be explainable and contestable, with humans able to understand how decisions are reached.

Assembly - The United Earth Assembly (UEA), primary legislative body representing all people of Earth.

B

Bicameral - Having two legislative chambers; in this context, referring to evolution toward a two-house global legislature.

Biodiversity Hotspots - Geographic areas with significant levels of biodiversity that are threatened by human activities.

C

Citizen Jury - A group of 100-200 randomly selected citizens who deliberate on specific policy issues and provide recommendations.

Citizens' Integrity Chamber (CIC) - A rotating panel of lottery-selected citizens who review high-level government decisions for integrity and public interest compliance.

Collective Rights - Rights held by groups, particularly indigenous communities, to preserve their heritage, language, traditions, and territories.

Commons - Natural resources and systems that are shared by all humanity rather than owned by individuals or corporations.

Continental Federations - Regional groupings of nations for coordination and governance at the continental level.

D

Deliberative Democracy - A form of democracy that emphasizes deliberation, reflection, and reason-giving in decision-making processes.

Digital Deliberation Platforms - Online systems that enable citizens to discuss, debate, and collaborate on policy issues.

Digital Inclusion - Ensuring that all people have access to and can effectively use digital technologies and internet services.

E

Ecocide - Large-scale environmental destruction that causes widespread or long-term damage to ecosystems.

Environmental Rights - Legal protections ensuring access to clean water, air, food, and a healthy environment.

Ethics Veto - Citizen-initiated mechanisms to block government decisions that violate ethical principles or public interest.

External Costs - Negative impacts of economic activities that are not reflected in market prices, such as pollution or resource depletion.

F

Foundational Charter - The initial operating agreement that establishes pilot programs and basic structures for the World Constitution.

Future Generations - People who will live after current generations, whose rights and interests must be protected in current decision-making.

G

Global Anti-Corruption Tribunal - A judicial body that handles cross-border corruption cases and ensures international compliance with anti-corruption standards.

Global Commons - Shared resources and systems that belong to all humanity, including atmosphere, oceans, biodiversity, and polar regions.

Global Council - The executive body that implements Assembly decisions with rotating regional representation.

Global Investigative Journalism Trust - An independent organization with protected funding that supports cross-border investigative reporting.

Global Peacekeeping Service - A volunteer-based force that intervenes in cases of genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and ecocide.

Global Referenda - Direct votes by all citizens on major policy questions, requiring significant participation and approval thresholds.

Governance - The systems and processes by which decisions are made and implemented in society.

H

Hybrid Conventions - Meetings that combine national delegates with randomly selected citizens to address major policy questions.

I

Inalienable Rights - Fundamental rights that cannot be taken away, suspended, or limited under any circumstances.

Innovation Grants - Funding provided to support new technologies, approaches, and solutions to global challenges.

Intergenerational Responsibility - The obligation to consider and protect the interests of future generations in current decision-making.

L

Localized Autonomy - The right of local communities and nations to govern their internal affairs without external interference.

O

Open-Source - Software, algorithms, or knowledge that is freely available for anyone to use, modify, and distribute.

Open-Access - Scientific research and publications that are available to everyone without financial barriers.

P

Participatory Democracy - A system where citizens actively participate in decision-making processes beyond just voting.

Participatory Democracy Mechanisms - Tools and processes that enable direct citizen involvement in governance.

Peaceful Resolution - The requirement that all international disputes must be resolved through negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or judicial settlement.

Planetary Boundaries - Scientific limits within which humanity can safely operate to avoid catastrophic environmental changes.

Planetary Resource Dividend - Universal basic income or services funded by fees on the use of global commons resources.

Popular Impeachment - Citizen-initiated processes for removing corrupt or ineffective officials from office.

Progressive Global Taxation - A tax system where rates increase with ability to pay, applied globally to reduce inequality.

Public Ledger Systems - Immutable cryptographic records that track government finances, decisions, and asset declarations.

R

Radical Transparency - The principle that all government operations, contracts, and decisions must be publicly accessible and understandable.

Referenda - Direct votes by citizens on specific policy questions or constitutional amendments.

Resource Fees - Charges imposed on the use or extraction of global commons resources.

Rotating Facilitation - Leadership systems where power and responsibilities rotate among participants rather than being held permanently by individuals or groups.

S

Shared Sovereignty - A redefinition of sovereignty as shared responsibility for the common good rather than absolute national authority.

Specialized Agencies - Organizations focused on specific areas like health, environment, science, social development, etc.

Subsidiarity - The principle that decisions should be made at the lowest competent level closest to affected people.

Sustainable Development - Development that meets present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

T

Technology Transfer - The sharing of technologies, knowledge, and capabilities between nations or organizations.

Transitional Implementation - The process of moving from current systems to the new World Constitution framework over time.

Treaties - Formal agreements between nations that establish rights, obligations, and cooperation mechanisms.

U

United Earth Assembly (UEA) - The primary global legislative body representing all people of Earth.

Universal Jurisdiction - The principle that certain crimes (like genocide, war crimes) can be prosecuted anywhere regardless of where they were committed.

Universal Rights - Rights that apply equally to all human beings regardless of nationality, culture, or other characteristics.

Unconditional Rights - Rights that cannot be suspended or limited under any circumstances, even during emergencies.

V

Veto Power - The ability of a single entity to block decisions; specifically prohibited in the World Constitution system.

Virtual Reality Democracy - Technology-enabled systems that allow citizens to participate in governance regardless of geographic location.

Whistleblower Protection - Legal safeguards and support systems for people who report corruption, illegal activities, or violations of public trust.

World Assembly - See United Earth Assembly (UEA).

World Court - The global judicial body that adjudicates international disputes and ensures compliance with constitutional provisions.

World Constitutional Convention - Regular gatherings to review the constitution's effectiveness and recommend improvements.

This glossary is intended to help citizens understand and engage with the World Constitution. For additional clarification or discussion of specific terms, please refer to the relevant sections of the constitution or contact your local citizen assembly.
