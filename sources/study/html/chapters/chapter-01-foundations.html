Chapter I: Fundamental Principles

Article 1: Human Dignity and Universal Rights

All human beings possess inherent dignity and equal rights that are unconditional and cannot be suspended or limited. No person shall be subjected to discrimination based on race, ethnicity, gender identity or expression, religion, sexual orientation, nationality, disability, age, language, or any other characteristic. These rights extend to collective rights for indigenous communities to preserve their heritage, language, traditions, and territories.

Gender Diversity Integration: Recognizing that gender expression manifests across natural human diversity, this Constitution adopts an integration approach. Gender identity shall be neither promoted nor suppressed by public policy. All individuals shall be integrated and recognized as equal human beings with full rights and dignity, allowing natural diversity to flourish without artificial social constraints. Every person has has right to determine their own gender identity with state recognition and protection from discrimination based on gender diversity.

Article 2: Shared Sovereignty and Subsidiarity

Sovereignty is redefined as shared responsibility for the common good. Nations retain authority over local and cultural affairs while committing to global cooperation. Decisions shall be made at the lowest competent level (principle of subsidiarity), with global governance addressing only issues that cannot be effectively handled locally or regionally. No single entity shall possess veto power over global decisions.

Article 3: Peace, Security, and Dispute Resolution

War and armed conflict are not permitted. If aggression is imminent, World Assembly may call on any and all to force end of conflict. All international disputes shall be resolved through negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or judicial settlement.

Article 3a: Disarmement

Mandatory, phased disarmament of weapons of mass destruction shall be implemented under international verification. Gradual wind down of military, to internationally low level standards for global peacekeeping.

Article 3A: Global Communication and Language

Primary International Language: To enable maximum participation in global matters, English shall be primary language for international affairs and inter-area communications, ensuring effective and accessible dialogue between all regions.

Scope Limitation: The language requirement applies only to inter-area communications between different linguistic regions. Local and regional affairs may be conducted in any language chosen by community.

Language Diversity Protection: The language of any tribe, area, or country shall be fully accepted and protected by this Constitution without hindrance. All local languages shall be preserved as cultural heritage with educational support for multilingual competence.

Article 4: Diversity as Global Strength

Diversity Principle: Diversity is recognized as ultimate strength of humanity. In nature, survival of any species or individual is a remarkable event. Diversity is phenomenon that allows and promotes survival and thriving of any entity in complex, ever-changing environments.

Diversity Protection: Global governance shall actively promote and protect diversity in all forms - biological, cultural, linguistic, ideological, and cognitive - as essential for human resilience, adaptability, and progress.

Unity in Diversity: While celebrating our differences as sources of collective strength, we shall recognize that our shared humanity provides foundation for cooperation and mutual understanding across all boundaries.

Article 5: Religious Freedom and Secular Governance

Individual Spiritual Freedom: Every person has right to practice their religion or spirituality individually and in community, with freedom of conscience, belief, and access to religious institutions without interference.

Secular Global Governance: No religion shall influence official world affairs or global governance. Religious doctrine shall be separate from political decision-making at the international level, ensuring policies serve all humanity regardless of spiritual beliefs.

No Promotion or Suppression: There shall be no force or incentive to suppress religious practice, and no force or incentive to propagate religion through state mechanisms or public policy.

Inter-Religious Relations: Interactions between religious entities shall be confined to negotiated outcomes and mutual respect, with the state serving as neutral mediator in disputes while protecting religious freedom for all.

Cultural and Spiritual Boundaries: Acknowledging that religious boundaries often coincide with cultural and linguistic boundaries formed by historical human interpretation of divine will, global governance shall approach religious diversity with humility, accepting that no single interpretation holds exclusive access to truth in matters of global governance. Individual spiritual needs are distinguished from effects on collective decision-making.
